TravelHelper — AI Chatbot for Travel Decision Support

Designed an AI-assisted travel booking experience that reduces decision friction through conversational guidance, recommendation flows, and human fallback support.

👤 Role

Product / Business Analysis

⌛️ Timeline

4 weeks

🛠️ Tools

Figma, Miro

🎯 Focus

Conversational UX, BPMN, Service Design

1. Business Problem

Most travel booking platforms I've used have the same problem: too many filters, too many tabs, too many choices thrown at you at once.

That leads to:

  • People spending 20–30 minutes just comparing flights
  • Opening 5–6 tabs for hotels, visa, weather, transport
  • Getting stuck when something unexpected happens
  • Waiting hours for support to reply

The business impact isn't subtle: booking abandonment, decision fatigue, and unnecessary support tickets.

I didn't want to build another booking form. I wanted to build something that actually helps people decide.

2. User Pain Points

😵

Decision Fatigue

Users face too many travel options and struggle to compare information efficiently.

⏱

Time-Consuming Planning

Searching flights, hotels, visas, and weather separately creates friction.

❓

Support Frustration

Users become frustrated when systems cannot handle unclear requests or exceptions.

3. Analysis Table

Beyond the surface-level pain points, I broke down each user problem into: how it actually affects the experience, and what product response I built to solve it.

User / Business Problem Impact on Experience Product / Service Response
Users feel overwhelmed by too many travel options Decision fatigue and slower booking decisions Guided conversational prompts: destination, budget, purpose
Unclear or incomplete inputs User frustration and drop-off risk Validation and re-prompt flow, no silent failure
Payment failures during checkout Booking abandonment Retry logic + alternative method suggestion
Lack of personalized recommendations Lower engagement and conversion Recommendation-based filtering via conversation, not search bars
Users require support beyond chatbot capability Increased operational friction Human handover workflow, escalation without dead-end

4. Product Thinking

Product Goal

TravelHelper was designed to:

  • simplify travel planning
  • reduce cognitive overload
  • guide users through booking decisions
  • provide support escalation when needed

Product Hypothesis

If users receive guided recommendations through conversational interaction, they will make travel decisions faster and with greater confidence — without feeling like they're “working” to book a trip.

I didn't just want a chatbot. I wanted a decision-support system disguised as a conversation.

5. Solution Overview

TravelHelper combines:

  • conversational guidance
  • recommendation logic
  • booking support
  • fallback recovery
  • human escalation

It treats travel planning as a dialogue, not a form.

6. System Flow / BPMN

This is where the BA side of the project really comes out.

Instead of just designing screens, I mapped the entire booking journey using standard BPMN notation with two explicit pools:

Pool Role
User Actions and decisions made by the traveler
TravelHelper System Backend logic, validation, recommendations, and error handling

This separation is important because it forces clarity between what the user does and what the system does — which is exactly what a Business Analyst does daily.

What the workflow includes:

  • Input validation before generating recommendations
  • Re-prompt loop instead of dead-end, keeping the conversation alive
  • Retry logic for payment failures, reducing abandonment
  • Human escalation path through a hybrid support model
  • Positive closure through confirmation and email

Why this matters

Most portfolios show pretty screens. This workflow shows I actually think about how the system behaves when things go wrong:

  • where things break
  • what happens after a failed payment
  • when the system gives up and calls a human

7. UX & Interface Design

I didn't build a chatbot with open-ended questions. That fails immediately.

Principle What I did
Guided Interaction Users click, choose, confirm — not type paragraphs
Conversational Simplicity Short prompts. Structured choices. No walls of text
Emotional Design Friendly tone. Light emoji use. Not robotic. Because travel is stressful enough already

8. Error Handling & Human Support

This part sounds boring, but recruiters actually love it.

TravelHelper handles three common failure cases:

Failure Case System Response
Unclear user input Retry with simpler options through a re-prompt loop
Failed payment Retry logic + alternative method suggestion
Unsupported request Graceful handover to human consultant

Business impact:

  • less user frustration
  • fewer abandoned bookings
  • lower support load for simple requests

I didn't just design the “happy path.” I designed the recovery paths.

9. Reflection

This project changed how I think about product work.

Key learnings:

  • Translating vague user pain into structured workflows is harder than it looks
  • A good BA doesn't just document — they close the loop between UX and operations
  • Exception handling is not an afterthought. It's half the value.

I walked away from this not with a “nice assignment,” but with a clear philosophy:

A product is only as good as what happens when it breaks.
